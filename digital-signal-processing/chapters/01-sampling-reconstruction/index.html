§ 1.1 Continuous vs Discrete Signals

Sampling model, impulse train, spectrum of sampled signal

A continuous-time signal $x(t)$ is defined for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. Sampling converts it to a discrete-time sequence $x[n] = x(nT_s)$ where $T_s$ is the sampling period and $f_s = 1/T_s$ is the sampling frequency.

We model ideal sampling as multiplication by an impulse train:

Ideal Sampling $$x_s(t) = x(t) \cdot p(t) = x(t) \cdot \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(t - nT_s)$$

Since $p(t)$ is periodic with period $T_s$, its Fourier series is $p(t) = \frac{1}{T_s}\sum_{k} e^{jk\Omega_s t}$ where $\Omega_s = 2\pi/T_s$. The spectrum of the sampled signal becomes:

Sampled Spectrum $$X_s(j\Omega) = \frac{1}{T_s}\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} X\!\left(j(\Omega - k\Omega_s)\right)$$

This is the fundamental result: the spectrum of the sampled signal is a sum of shifted copies of the original spectrum, each centered at integer multiples of $\Omega_s$. This spectral replication is the basis for understanding both perfect reconstruction and aliasing.

Step 1

Express the product in the frequency domain using the convolution property of the Fourier transform: $X_s(j\Omega) = \frac{1}{2\pi} X(j\Omega) * P(j\Omega)$

Step 2

The Fourier transform of the impulse train is itself an impulse train: $P(j\Omega) = \Omega_s \sum_k \delta(\Omega - k\Omega_s)$

Step 3

Substituting and using the sifting property of $\delta$: $X_s(j\Omega) = \frac{1}{T_s}\sum_k X(j(\Omega - k\Omega_s))$

§ 1.2 Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem

Perfect reconstruction conditions and sinc interpolation

Theorem (Nyquist-Shannon): If $x(t)$ is bandlimited with $X(j\Omega) = 0$ for $|\Omega| > \Omega_{\max}$, then $x(t)$ can be perfectly reconstructed from its samples $x[n] = x(nT_s)$ if and only if:

Nyquist Criterion $$\Omega_s > 2\Omega_{\max} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad f_s > 2f_{\max}$$

The minimum sampling rate $f_s = 2f_{\max}$ is the Nyquist rate. The frequency $f_s/2$ is the Nyquist frequency — the highest frequency representable without aliasing.

Proof: When $\Omega_s > 2\Omega_{\max}$, the shifted copies $X(j(\Omega - k\Omega_s))$ do not overlap. Thus, $X(j\Omega)$ can be recovered by applying an ideal lowpass filter with cutoff $\Omega_s/2$ and gain $T_s$:

$$X(j\Omega) = T_s \cdot X_s(j\Omega) \cdot H_{\text{LP}}(j\Omega), \quad H_{\text{LP}}(j\Omega) = \begin{cases} 1, & |\Omega| \le \Omega_s/2 \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

The reconstruction formula in the time domain is the sinc interpolation:

Sinc Reconstruction $$x(t) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} x[n] \cdot \operatorname{sinc}\!\left(\frac{t - nT_s}{T_s}\right)$$ (1.2)
Derivation

Taking the inverse Fourier transform of $X(j\Omega) = T_s X_s(j\Omega) H_{\text{LP}}(j\Omega)$ and noting that the time-domain equivalent of the ideal LPF is a sinc function: $h_{\text{LP}}(t) = \operatorname{sinc}(t/T_s)$, the convolution yields the sinc interpolation formula directly.

§ 1.3 Aliasing

Spectral overlap, aliased frequency formula, anti-aliasing filters

When $\Omega_s < 2\Omega_{\max}$, the shifted spectral copies overlap — this is aliasing. The overlapped components are indistinguishable from lower-frequency content, causing permanent distortion that no post-processing can remove.

Aliased Frequency $$f_{\text{alias}} = \left|f_{\text{sig}} - \operatorname{round}\!\left(\frac{f_{\text{sig}}}{f_s}\right) \cdot f_s\right|$$

An anti-aliasing filter is a lowpass filter placed before the sampler. It must satisfy:

  • Passband: $|H(j\Omega)| \approx 1$ for $|\Omega| \le 2\pi f_{\max}$
  • Stopband: $|H(j\Omega)| \approx 0$ for $|\Omega| \ge \pi f_s$ (i.e., $f_s/2$)

Practical examples:

  • Audio CD: $f_s = 44.1$ kHz, $f_{\max} = 20$ kHz, ratio $= 2.205\times$
  • Telephone: $f_s = 8$ kHz, $f_{\max} = 3.4$ kHz, ratio $= 2.35\times$
  • Stroboscopic effect: Wagon wheels appearing to spin backward when the frame rate aliases the spoke frequency

§ 1.4 Quantization

Uniform quantization, SQNR, dithering

After sampling, each sample value must be represented with a finite number of bits $B$. Uniform quantization divides the input range $[x_{\min}, x_{\max}]$ into $2^B$ equal intervals of width:

Step Size $$\Delta = \frac{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}{2^B}$$

The quantization error $e[n] = x[n] - \hat{x}[n]$ is bounded by $|e[n]| \le \Delta/2$. Under the assumption that $e[n]$ is uniformly distributed, white, and uncorrelated with the signal:

SQNR $$\text{SQNR} \approx 6.02B + 1.76 \text{ dB}$$ (1.4)
Derivation

For a full-scale sinusoid, signal power $= A^2/2$ where $A = 2^{B-1}\Delta$. Noise power of uniform distribution on $[-\Delta/2, \Delta/2]$ is $\sigma_e^2 = \Delta^2/12$. Thus $\text{SQNR} = 10\log_{10}(A^2/2 \cdot 12/\Delta^2) = 10\log_{10}(3 \cdot 2^{2B}/2) = 6.02B + 1.76$ dB.

Dithering adds a small amount of white noise (typically $\pm\Delta/2$) before quantization. This randomizes the quantization error, converting harmonic distortion into broadband noise — which is perceptually less objectionable.

§ 1.5 Reconstruction

Ideal LPF, zero-order hold, aperture effect

Ideal reconstruction uses multiplication by $T_s$ followed by an ideal lowpass filter with cutoff $\Omega_s/2$ and gain $T_s$. This is equivalent to sinc interpolation — but physically unrealizable since sinc is non-causal and infinite.

Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) is the simplest practical D/A converter. Each sample is held constant for one sample period:

$$x_{\text{ZOH}}(t) = \sum_n x[n] \cdot \operatorname{rect}\!\left(\frac{t - nT_s - T_s/2}{T_s}\right)$$

The ZOH frequency response introduces the aperture effect:

ZOH Transfer Function $$H_{\text{ZOH}}(j\Omega) = T_s \cdot \operatorname{sinc}\!\left(\frac{\Omega T_s}{2\pi}\right) \cdot e^{-j\Omega T_s/2}$$

The sinc envelope causes high-frequency attenuation (droop) that must be compensated. A first-order hold uses linear interpolation between samples, producing a triangular pulse shape with transfer function $H_{\text{FOH}} \propto \operatorname{sinc}^2(\Omega T_s / 2\pi)$.

Key Equations Reference

Ideal Sampling
$$x_s(t) = x(t)\sum_n \delta(t-nT_s)$$

Sampling is modeled as multiplication by an impulse train with period $T_s$.

Time Domain

Derivation: The ideal sampler extracts signal values at integer multiples of $T_s$. The delta function property $x(t)\delta(t-t_0) = x(t_0)\delta(t-t_0)$ ensures each impulse carries the signal amplitude at that instant.

Nyquist-Shannon Theorem
$$f_s > 2f_{\max}$$

Perfect reconstruction requires sampling above twice the maximum frequency.

Frequency DomainDesign

Derivation: Sampling replicates the spectrum at multiples of $f_s$. When $f_s > 2f_{\max}$, replicas don't overlap and the original spectrum is recoverable via ideal lowpass filtering.

Sinc Interpolation
$$x(t) = \sum_n x[n]\,\mathrm{sinc}\!\left(\frac{t-nT_s}{T_s}\right)$$

Exact reconstruction by weighting each sample with a shifted sinc function.

Time Domain

Derivation: The inverse Fourier transform of the ideal rectangular lowpass filter is the sinc function. The reconstruction is a convolution of the sampled signal with this sinc kernel.

SQNR
$$\text{SQNR} \approx 6.02B + 1.76 \text{ dB}$$

Each additional bit of resolution adds approximately 6 dB of signal-to-noise ratio.

Design

Derivation: For a full-scale sine wave with $B$-bit uniform quantization, signal power is $2^{2B}\Delta^2/8$ and noise power is $\Delta^2/12$, yielding $10\log_{10}(3\cdot 2^{2B}/2)$.

Chapter Quiz

1. A 15 kHz signal is sampled at 24 kHz. What is the aliased frequency?

15 kHz (no aliasing)
9 kHz
6 kHz
3 kHz
The Nyquist rate is 30 kHz, but $f_s = 24$ kHz < 30 kHz, so aliasing occurs. $f_{\text{alias}} = |15 - \text{round}(15/24)\cdot 24| = |15 - 24| = 9$ kHz. The 15 kHz tone appears as a 9 kHz component.

2. How many quantization levels are available with 10-bit resolution?

100
512
1024
2048
With $B$ bits, there are $2^B = 2^{10} = 1024$ quantization levels. SQNR ≈ 6.02(10) + 1.76 = 61.96 dB.

3. What is the primary purpose of an anti-aliasing filter?

Remove frequency components above $f_s/2$ before sampling
Boost low-frequency components after reconstruction
Reduce quantization noise in the passband
Compensate for zero-order hold droop
An anti-aliasing filter is a lowpass filter applied before sampling that eliminates frequency components above the Nyquist frequency $f_s/2$. Without it, those components alias into the baseband and cannot be separated from the desired signal.
