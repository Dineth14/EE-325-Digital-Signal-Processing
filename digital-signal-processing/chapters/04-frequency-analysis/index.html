Frequency-Domain Analysis

DTFT, DFT, and the FFT — transform time-domain sequences into the frequency domain and back.

Theory

▶

§4.1 The Discrete-Time Fourier Transform (DTFT)

Continuous frequency representation of discrete signals

The DTFT maps a discrete-time sequence to a continuous function of frequency $\omega$:

$$X(e^{j\omega})=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty}x[n]\,e^{-j\omega n}$$

The inverse DTFT recovers $x[n]$:

$$x[n]=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X(e^{j\omega})\,e^{j\omega n}\,d\omega$$

Properties:

  • $X(e^{j\omega})$ is periodic with period $2\pi$.
  • If $x[n]$ is real, then $|X(e^{j\omega})|$ is even and $\angle X(e^{j\omega})$ is odd.
  • Parseval's theorem: $\sum_n |x[n]|^2 = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}|X(e^{j\omega})|^2 d\omega$.

The DTFT is the Z-transform evaluated on the unit circle: $X(e^{j\omega})=X(z)|_{z=e^{j\omega}}$, which exists whenever the ROC of $X(z)$ includes $|z|=1$.

▶

§4.2 The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)

Sampled DTFT for finite-length sequences

The DFT is a sampled version of the DTFT, suitable for computation on finite-length sequences:

$$X[k]=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x[n]\,e^{-j2\pi kn/N}=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x[n]\,W_N^{kn}$$

where $W_N=e^{-j2\pi/N}$ is the $N$th root of unity (twiddle factor).

$$x[n]=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}X[k]\,e^{j2\pi kn/N}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}X[k]\,W_N^{-kn}$$

Key relationships:

  • $X[k]=X(e^{j\omega})|_{\omega=2\pi k/N}$ — the DFT samples the DTFT at $N$ equally-spaced points.
  • Circular convolution: $x_1[n]\circledast x_2[n] \leftrightarrow X_1[k]\cdot X_2[k]$ — multiplication in DFT domain = circular convolution in time.
  • Zero-padding to length $L>N$ interpolates the DTFT between DFT samples (does NOT increase resolution).
▶

§4.3 The Discrete Fourier Series (DFS)

Periodic extension and DFS coefficients

The DFS represents a periodic sequence $\tilde{x}[n]$ with period $N$ as a sum of complex exponentials:

$$\tilde{X}[k]=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\tilde{x}[n]\,e^{-j2\pi kn/N}$$
$$\tilde{x}[n]=\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}\tilde{X}[k]\,e^{j2\pi kn/N}$$

The DFS coefficients $\tilde{X}[k]$ are themselves periodic with period $N$. The DFT of a finite-length sequence can be viewed as the DFS of its periodic extension.

▶

§4.4 The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)

Cooley-Tukey radix-2 DIT algorithm

The Cooley-Tukey radix-2 decimation-in-time algorithm computes the DFT in $O(N\log_2 N)$ instead of $O(N^2)$:

  1. Split $x[n]$ into even-indexed and odd-indexed subsequences.
  2. Compute $N/2$-point DFTs of each half.
  3. Combine using the butterfly operation:
    $$X[k]=G[k]+W_N^k\,H[k]$$$$X[k+N/2]=G[k]-W_N^k\,H[k]$$
    where $G[k]$ and $H[k]$ are the DFTs of the even and odd halves.

Computational savings for $N=1024$: DFT requires $N^2=1{,}048{,}576$ operations vs FFT's $N\log_2 N=10{,}240$ — a 100× speedup.

▶

§4.5 Spectral Leakage & Windowing

Window effects, sidelobes, and resolution tradeoffs

When a finite window truncates an infinite signal, the DFT sees the product $x_w[n]=x[n]\cdot w[n]$. In the frequency domain this becomes convolution:

$$X_w(e^{j\omega})=\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}X(e^{j\theta})\,W(e^{j(\omega-\theta)})\,d\theta$$

The main lobe width of $W(e^{j\omega})$ determines frequency resolution; sidelobes cause spectral leakage. Common tradeoffs:

  • Rectangular: Narrowest main lobe, highest sidelobes (−13 dB).
  • Hanning: 2× main lobe, −31 dB sidelobes.
  • Hamming: Similar to Hanning, −42 dB sidelobes.
  • Blackman: 3× main lobe, −58 dB sidelobes.
  • Kaiser: Adjustable via parameter $\beta$.

Key Equations

DTFT

$$X(e^{j\omega})=\sum_n x[n]e^{-j\omega n}$$

Continuous frequency representation of a discrete signal. Periodic in $2\pi$.

DFT

$$X[k]=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}x[n]W_N^{kn}$$

$N$ samples of the DTFT. Computable and the basis for all digital spectral analysis.

FFT Butterfly

$$X[k]=G[k]+W_N^k H[k]$$

Core operation of the Cooley-Tukey algorithm. Reduces complexity from $O(N^2)$ to $O(N\log N)$.

Parseval's Theorem

$$\sum|x[n]|^2=\frac{1}{N}\sum|X[k]|^2$$

Energy is preserved in both domains. Verifiable numerically as a DFT sanity check.

Chapter Quiz

1. An N-point DFT of a signal zero-padded to length 2N:

Doubles the frequency resolution
Halves spectral leakage
Interpolates the DTFT between original DFT bins
Changes the signal's spectral content
Zero-padding does not add new information or increase resolution. It provides denser sampling of the DTFT, effectively interpolating between the original $N$ DFT bins.

2. The FFT computes a 1024-point DFT with approximately how many complex multiplications?

1,048,576
5,120
10,240
1,024
The radix-2 FFT requires $(N/2)\log_2 N = (1024/2)\times 10 = 5{,}120$ complex multiplications, compared to $N^2 = 1{,}048{,}576$ for the direct DFT.

3. The Hamming window reduces spectral leakage by:

Suppressing sidelobes at the cost of a wider main lobe
Zero-padding the signal
Increasing the sampling rate
Applying a high-pass filter
The Hamming window tapers the signal to reduce discontinuities at the edges, which suppresses sidelobes (−42 dB) at the cost of a wider main lobe compared to the rectangular window.
